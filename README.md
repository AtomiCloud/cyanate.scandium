# cyanate.scandium

## Cyanate

Cyanate (OCN-) is a polyatomic anion composed of one oxygen, one carbon, and one nitrogen atom bonded in sequence. It is the conjugate base of cyanic acid (HOCN). Cyanate salts such as sodium cyanate (NaOCN) are white crystalline solids that are soluble in water. In 1828, Friedrich Wohler accidentally synthesized urea by heating silver cyanate with ammonium chloride — this was the first synthesis of an organic compound from inorganic precursors, a landmark moment in chemistry that challenged vitalism.

Cyanate is not to be confused with its isomer, fulminate (CNO-), which is explosive. Cyanate is comparatively well-behaved.

## Scandium

Scandium (Sc) is a chemical element with atomic number 21. It is a soft, silvery-white transition metal that tarnishes in air and burns easily when ignited. Despite being classified as a rare earth element, scandium is actually more abundant in the Earth's crust than lead, mercury, or gold — it is just rarely found in concentrated deposits, making extraction uneconomical for most applications.

Scandium was predicted by Mendeleev in 1869 as "eka-boron" and discovered by Lars Fredrik Nilson in 1879 in the Scandinavian minerals euxenite and gadolinite, hence the name.

Its primary uses include scandium-aluminium alloys for aerospace components, solid oxide fuel cells, and stadium lighting. A scandium-aluminium baseball bat was briefly popular in the early 2000s before being deemed too effective.
